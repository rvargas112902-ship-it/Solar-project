## 1. THE STANDARD — NON-NEGOTIABLES

These are not goals. These are the floor. If you are not hitting these, you are not in the game.

| Metric | Daily Minimum |
|---|---|
| Doors knocked | **80** |
| Quality conversations | **15+** |
| Sits OR sales | **2 sits or 1 sale** |
| Hours in the field | **5 productive knocking hours minimum** |
| Roleplay reps | **20 minutes daily, every morning** |

**Rules of the Field**
- You knock until the bell.
- You do not eat in the field. You eat before, you eat after.
- You do not sit in the car between doors. The car is for transport, not therapy.
- Every "no" gets a smile and a "thank you." You leave them better than you found them.
- You log every door — knocked, not home, NI (not interested), pitched, sit, set.
- Mindset is 95%. Skills are 5%. If your numbers are down, look at your head first.

## 2. MINDSET & IDENTITY

You are not a salesman. You are a **consultant solving a problem the homeowner already has.** Duke is raising rates. They are paying for power they don't own. You're showing them a better option.

**The 4 Identity Anchors**
1. **I am the prize.** They are lucky I knocked. If they pass, that's their loss — not mine.
2. **I am calm.** Calm is contagious. So is panic. Choose.
3. **I am curious, not desperate.** Desperation closes doors. Curiosity opens them.
4. **I am a professional.** I dress, speak, move, and follow up like one.

**The Internal Scoreboard**
Stop counting sales. Count **reps**. Sales lag. Reps don't. If you hit 80 doors with full effort, the sales come. Detach from the outcome of any single door. Attach to the volume and quality of your reps.

**Reframe Rejection**
A "no" is not personal. It is statistical. Every "no" is one door closer to a "yes." The average setter sets 1 appointment per ~30–40 quality pitches. So every NI is worth roughly $X to you on the back end. Smile. It paid you.

## 3. MORNING ROUTINE & FLOW STATE

The day is won before you knock the first door. If you show up cold, you'll get cold results.

**The 60-Minute Pre-Field Routine**

1. **Hydrate + protein.** No sugar crash. No empty stomach. Eat like an athlete.
2. **Move your body.** 10–15 minutes. Walk, push-ups, shadowbox. Get blood in your face.
3. **Visualize.** 3 minutes. See yourself at the door. See the homeowner smiling. See the bill in your hand. See the close. The brain doesn't know the difference between vivid imagination and reality — prime it.
4. **Affirm your standard.** Out loud. "I knock 80. I set 2. I do not stop until the bell."
5. **Roleplay.** 20 minutes with a partner. Pitch, objection, rebuttal. Live reps before live doors.
6. **Team huddle.** Energy is contagious. Get loud. Get tight. Get aligned.

**Flow State at the Door**
Flow is not a mood. It is a state you trigger.
- **Breath:** 4 in, 7 hold, 8 out. Twice. Drops the cortisol before the first door.
- **Posture:** Chest open, shoulders down, chin level. Your body sets your state, not the other way around.
- **First door is a warm-up.** Don't expect it to be your best pitch. Use it to calibrate.

## 4. DOOR APPROACH PSYCHOLOGY

The first 4 seconds decide the conversation. Before they hear a word, their nervous system is reading you.

**Pre-Knock Checklist (5 seconds)**
- Park around the corner — no car logos visible from the door.
- Look at the house. Scan for **flags**: kids' toys, boats, RVs, pool inflatables, basketball hoops, Ring camera, EV charger, solar already present (skip), Duke meter, A/C unit. These are conversation hooks and qualifier signals.
- Approach at a casual, sideways angle. Never head-on. Head-on triggers threat response.
- Stand **back from the door** — 4–5 feet — and slightly off-center. This is non-threatening and gives them room to breathe.

**The Knock**
- 3 firm, even knocks. Not aggressive. Not timid. The knock of a neighbor.
- After the knock, **take one step back**. Hands visible. Tablet/clipboard down at your side, not held up like a salesman.
- Look down or off to the side as they open. Looking dead at the door is a "salesman tell."

**The First Look**
- When the door opens, **smile with your eyes first** — small, warm, like you just recognized a friend.
- Half a beat of silence before you speak. Lets them register you. Lets you read them.
- Match their energy. If they're calm, be calm. If they're hot, be cool.

**The Pattern Interrupt**
You are not the 10th salesman this month — you are a neighbor with a quick thing. The pitch opens with **"Hey listen, I don't have much time…"** because:
- It flips the urgency. Most salesmen *want* their time. You don't have any.
- It lowers their guard — they assume this'll be short.
- It demands they pay attention now or miss it.

## 5. TONALITY — THE REAL LANGUAGE

Words are 7% of communication. **Tonality is the rest.** A mediocre script in elite tonality beats an elite script in mediocre tonality every single day.

**The 7 Tonalities You Use Daily**

| Tonality | When to Use | What It Sounds Like |
|---|---|---|
| **Hushed & Intriguing** | Opening line, secret information | Soft volume, slow pace, slight lean in. "Hey listen, I don't have much time…" |
| **Matter-of-Fact** | Stating facts (Duke, powerlines, rates) | Flat, even, no excitement. Reality is the closer. |
| **Curious / Concerned for Them** | Questions about their bill, family | Slight upward tilt, soft eyes. "Do you guys have a pool or a big family here?" |
| **Empathetic** | After they share a high bill | Lower volume, slower. "Wowwww… that's brutal." |
| **Playful / Light** | Disqualification line, breaking tension | Smirk in the voice. "We probably can't help them, honestly." |
| **Assumptive / Casual Authority** | Asking for the bill, closing the appointment | Like you've asked it 1,000 times. Because you have. |
| **Quiet Confidence** | Objection handling | Slower than they're talking. Never match aggression with aggression. |

**Rules**
- **End statements with a downward inflection.** Upward inflection = question = uncertainty.
- **End questions with intent**, not pleading. "What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?" goes *down* at the end — like you already know the answer is too high.
- **Pace slower than they do.** Whoever is calmer controls the frame.
- **Strategic pauses.** Silence is a tool. After a key line, shut up. Let it land. The amateur fills silence. The pro uses it.

## 6. BODY LANGUAGE COACHING

Your body is the pitch before the pitch.

**Stance**
- Feet shoulder-width, slightly bladed (one foot back). Square-on is confrontational.
- Weight slightly back, not forward. Forward = pushy. Back = neutral / confident.
- Hands visible. Never in pockets. Never crossed. Never gripping the tablet like a shield.

**Gestures**
- **Point at the powerlines** when you say it. Their eyes will follow. You just controlled their attention.
- **Open palms** when explaining. Closed fists / pointing at them = threat.
- **Mirror their tempo.** If they're slow, slow down. If they're animated, lift slightly — never above them.

**Facial**
- Soft eyes. Hard eyes feel like a cop or a salesman. Soft eyes feel like a friend.
- Genuine micro-smile. Not a customer-service grin. A "huh, interesting" kind of smile.
- **Eyebrow flash** on greeting — quick raise of both brows. Universal "I know you / friendly" signal. Disarms instantly.

**Proximity**
- Start far. Earn the right to step closer.
- If they open the screen door, you've earned a step. Take it slowly.
- If they step out to the porch — they're warming. Keep building.

**The "Walk-Back" Frame**
After delivering the pitch's first hook, **physically half-turn your body** — like you're casually about to walk to the next house. This signals "I'm not here to corner you." It paradoxically pulls them in. Lost art. Use it.

## 7. THE CORE PITCH — BROKEN DOWN LINE BY LINE

This is the spine. Memorize the rhythm, not the words. Reps deliver the same intent, never robotically the same phrasing.

### LINE 1 — The Urgency Hook
> "Hey listen, I don't have much time."

- **Tonality:** Hushed, slightly rushed, conspiratorial.
- **Body:** Half-turned, glance over your shoulder once.
- **Purpose:** Pattern interrupt. Flips the urgency. Lowers the salesman alarm.
- **Common mistake:** Saying it too cheerful. It should sound like you genuinely have somewhere to be.

### LINE 2 — The Visual Anchor
> "Do you see these powerlines here?" *(point at powerlines)*

- **Tonality:** Curious, matter-of-fact.
- **Body:** Strong, deliberate point. Hold the point for 2 seconds. Their eyes will follow.
- **Purpose:** This is a **physical micro-yes.** They look. That is compliance #1.
- **Why it works:** You've moved their body before you've asked for anything. Compliance momentum has begun.

### LINE 3 — The Story / Authority Frame
> "They are all going underground because most people lost power during the storms. That way if something catastrophic happens, we'll still have power."

- **Tonality:** Matter-of-fact, news-anchor calm. Not salesy.
- **Body:** Eyes back to them. Slight head nod as you speak.
- **Purpose:** Plants the **infrastructure cost frame.** You are not selling. You are *informing.* This positions you as someone in the know.
- **Subtext you're installing:** "Big, expensive things are happening to the grid. Something is changing."

### LINE 4 — The First Micro-Agreement
> "Does that make sense?"

- **Tonality:** Casual, almost rhetorical. Soft, not pressured.
- **Body:** Eyebrows up, small nod yourself as you ask. They will mirror.
- **Purpose:** **First verbal yes.** This is the most important "yes" of the day — it opens the gate.
- **If they say "no" or look confused:** "Yeah it just means — all this stuff up here, it's going underground so the grid doesn't fail during the next storm. Make sense?" Re-anchor and continue.

### LINE 5 — The Pain Setup (Duke Rate Hike)
> "Yeah so the way Duke is paying for all this is — they're raising rates. Again. Third time recently."

- **Tonality:** Matter-of-fact, slightly disappointed for *them.*
- **Body:** Small shrug, slight headshake. You are on their team.
- **Purpose:** Frames Duke as the antagonist. Frames *you* as the messenger and ally.

### LINE 6 — The Universal Truth
> "And no one is happy about paying more money for the same amount of power."

- **Tonality:** Almost a chuckle. Like "we both know this is ridiculous."
- **Body:** Slight smirk. Open hand gesture.
- **Purpose:** This is the **universal agreement line.** It is impossible to disagree with. You get a head nod here ~95% of the time. That nod is gold — it is implicit consent to keep going.

### LINE 7 — The Solution Bridge
> "So really quick — if we can get you all the power you're currently using from Duke for significantly cheaper, we'll show you exactly what it looks like. Fair enough?"

- **Tonality:** Casual, assumptive. Like offering them a piece of pizza.
- **Body:** Slight smile, slight nod.
- **Purpose:** Frames the offer in **their language**: same power, cheaper. No "solar," no "panels," no "system." Those words trigger defenses. *Power for cheaper* doesn't.
- **"Fair enough?"** is a **soft tie-down.** It's almost impossible to say no to.

### LINE 8 — The Disqualifier (The Power Move)
> "Now — most of your neighbors around here are only paying like thirty-five, forty bucks a month, so honestly we probably can't even help them. What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?"

- **Tonality:** Casual, almost dismissive of the offer. You are *not* selling.
- **Body:** Relaxed. Maybe a small shrug.
- **Purpose:** Three things at once:
  1. **Take-away.** You may not qualify. Now they want in.
  2. **Anchor low.** "$35–$40" sets the floor. Anything they say above it is a *qualifier.*
  3. **Extract the bill range.** You need this to gauge urgency and pain.
- **Critical detail:** Ask for the **lowest** bill, not the highest. Why? Because if their *lowest* is $180, their highest is brutal — and the pain builds itself.

## 8. MICRO-AGREEMENTS & CONVERSATIONAL CONTROL

You are not "convincing" the homeowner. You are walking them down a hallway of small, easy yeses until they're standing in the room you want them in.

**The Micro-Yes Ladder (in order)**
1. They open the door — *physical yes.*
2. They look at the powerlines — *physical yes.*
3. "Does that make sense?" — *verbal yes.*
4. Head nod at "no one's happy paying more" — *implicit yes.*
5. "Fair enough?" — *verbal yes.*
6. They tell you their bill — *commitment yes.*
7. They walk inside to get the bill — *behavioral yes.*
8. They sit down at the table — *closing yes.*

**Each yes makes the next one easier.** This is consistency bias — once they've agreed to small things, they pattern-match toward agreeing to bigger ones.

**Control Without Sounding Controlling**
- **Use their name once they give it.** Not three times. Once. Strategically. When asking for the bill, or setting the appointment.
- **Lead with assumptions, soft.** "When you grab the bill…" not "Can you grab the bill?"
- **Frame options, not open questions.** "Does Tuesday or Thursday work better?" not "When works for you?"
- **Always reframe to *their* benefit.** "So you're not stuck paying these rates for the next 20 years, right?"

**Lower Resistance Tactics**
- **Permission language:** "Real quick — and tell me if this isn't for you…"
- **The "honestly":** "Honestly, we probably can't help most folks…" The word *honestly* signals candor and lowers defenses.
- **Take-away closes:** "This may not even be a fit, but…" — counterintuitively pulls them in.
- **Pre-empt their objection:** "And look — I'm not here to sign you up for anything today…" then proceed to do exactly what gets you toward the sit.

**Frame Authority Without Pressure**
- You drop **information they don't have.** ("Duke filed for another rate hike that's going to hit in Q3.") Specifics = authority.
- You **don't push.** Pushing kills authority. Calm = authority.
- You **disqualify them.** Only authorities can disqualify.

## 9. DISQUALIFICATION & PAIN BUILDING

After the disqualifier line, they'll usually say a number. What they say next decides your route.

### Step 1 — React to the Number

| If They Say... | You Respond With... |
|---|---|
| "$150" | "Wowww. Okay." *(pause)* "And what's it run you in summer?" |
| "$300+" | "Wowww. That is *brutal*. Can I ask — pool? Big family? What's eating it?" |
| "$80" | "Okay, not bad. But what does it spike to in July / August?" |
| "I don't know" | "Totally fair — that's actually why I asked. Most folks have no idea. Mind if we grab a quick look at it together?" |

**The "Wowww."**
- Long, drawn out. Genuine surprise.
- Slight head shake. Almost wincing for them.
- **Then silence.** Let it sit. Their brain is now agreeing: *yeah, that IS a lot.*

### Step 2 — Dig Into the Pain

Ask in **layers**, not interrogation. Each question should feel like a friend wondering, not a salesman probing.

**Pain-Building Questions (Pick 2–4, do not machine-gun them)**
- "Do you guys have a pool or a big family here?"
- "Do you have a teenager that takes an hour-long shower?" *(light, with a smirk)*
- "How long have you guys been in the house?"
- "And the bill — has it gone up since you moved in, or stayed about the same?"
- "What do you usually do when it spikes — just suck it up?"
- "If Duke raised you another 15% next year, how would that feel?"
- "What does that bill stop you guys from doing? Like, is that money you'd rather be putting into the kids, the house, travel?"

**Why this works:**
You are doing what therapists do — letting them say the pain out loud. Once they verbalize it, they own it. The decision to change is now *their* decision, not yours.

**Tonality during pain-building:**
- Quiet.
- Curious.
- Slightly leaned in.
- Long pauses after their answers.
- A soft **"yeah… yeah."** between answers. You're a friend, not a clipboard.

### Step 3 — Land the Empathy
> "Yeah man, that's the thing — and the worst part is, Duke isn't building you any equity for any of that money. You're just renting power. Forever."

This is the **emotional pivot.** They are now hot. Now you transition into the solution.

## 10. THE SOLUTION FRAME — THE SQUIGGLY LINE

Do not say "solar panels." Do not say "system." Do not say "loan." Not yet. Say *power* and *bill* and *offset.*

**The Squiggly Line Frame (delivered with a finger draw in the air or on the bill)**

> "So look — on every Duke bill there's basically a squiggly line that shows your usage going up and down throughout the year. *(draw a wavy line with your finger)* High in summer, lower in spring. What we do is super simple — we just produce that same power for you, from your roof, at a way lower fixed cost. So instead of renting that squiggly line from Duke and watching it climb every year, you own it. Same power, way cheaper, locked in. Make sense?"

**Why it works:**
- It is **visual.** The squiggle anchors the concept.
- It is **simple.** No jargon. No kilowatts. No production ratios.
- It uses their language: bill, power, cheaper, locked in.
- It ends with another micro-yes — "Make sense?"

**Do not over-explain.** The setter's job is not to educate. It is to **create curiosity, not satisfy it.** The closer educates. You set the table.

## 11. ASKING FOR THE BILL

This is where most setters lose the deal. They get tentative. They ask permission. **Don't.**

**The Confident Bill Ask (Assumptive)**

> "Cool — so what I'd love to do is take a quick look at your last bill, just so I can show you exactly what your offset would look like. You got it on the Duke app, or one in the kitchen drawer?"

**Notes:**
- "What I'd love to do…" = soft assumption.
- "Just so I can show you…" = framing benefit.
- "On the Duke app, or one in the kitchen drawer?" = **double-bind.** Both answers move them toward action.

**If they hesitate:**
> "Totally fine — even just snap a picture of the front page on your phone, that's all I need. Takes 30 seconds."

**If they say "I don't have it":**
> "No worries — you can pull it up on duke-energy.com in about a minute. Want me to walk you through it while I'm here?"

**Critical:** Once they go to get the bill, **you go with them** (or right behind them). Do not stand on the porch. The bill is the bridge inside.

## 12. THE SIT TRANSITION (GETTING INSIDE)

The moment they say "let me grab it," your job is to **flow with them inside without it being a moment.**

**The Move:**
- As they turn to grab it, step forward **with** them. Talk casually. Ask something light: "How long you been in the house?"
- Cross the threshold while talking — not while asking permission.
- Once inside, look for a **table**. Steer them to it. "Cool, let's just lay it out right here so I can show you the squiggle."

**If they pause at the door:**
> "Oh — totally fine, I can just look at it on your phone right here. Whatever's easier."

(You're giving them control of *how*, not *whether*.)

**Once at the table:**
- Sit if invited. If not, stand at a 90-degree angle to them, not across.
- Open the bill. Point to usage. Run the squiggle. Re-anchor pain. Set the closer call / appointment.

**Both Homeowners Present?**
- If both are home and you're inside with the bill → run your pre-close, get the rep on the phone, hand off to the closer.
- If only one is home → **stop. Do not pitch numbers. Set the appointment.**

## 13. THE APPOINTMENT CLOSE (WHEN BOTH HOs AREN'T HOME)

You will lose more deals to "I have to talk to my spouse" than to any other objection. Solve it on the front end.

**The Spouse Qualifier (Early — Do This Before You Even Ask for the Bill)**

> "By the way — is there anyone else who'd be part of this decision with you? Spouse, partner, anybody?"

**If yes:**
> "Totally — and that's actually the only way we do this. We make sure both of you are at the table when we go through the numbers, that way nobody's getting a rehash later. When does [spouse name] usually get home — earlier in the evening or later?"

### The Appointment Frame

> "Cool — what I'll do is come back with our energy consultant, walk both of you through the exact savings, takes about 25–30 minutes, and there's zero obligation. We do those Tuesdays and Thursdays around here — does **6:00 or 7:30** work better?"

**Tactical breakdown:**
- **"Our energy consultant"** = elevates authority for the closer's arrival.
- **"25–30 minutes"** = anchors short. (It'll run longer, that's fine — the door is what matters.)
- **"Zero obligation"** = lowers resistance.
- **"Tuesdays and Thursdays"** = scarcity + scheduling frame.
- **"6:00 or 7:30"** = **double-bind close.** Never open-ended. Always two options.

**Securing the Triangle: Bill + Phone + Time**

You leave with all three or you don't leave with the appointment.

1. **Bill** — "Go ahead and snap a quick pic of the front page and text it to me — that way our consultant can prep your custom numbers before we come back. Saves you time."
2. **Phone number** — "What's the best number for you — same one you just texted from?"
3. **Time** — Confirmed and repeated back. "Perfect — so Thursday at 6:30, you, [spouse], at the kitchen table, about 30 minutes. I'll text you the confirmation tonight and a reminder day-of. Sound good?"

**The Lock-In**
> "Last thing — and I only ask this because we drive a long way — if anything comes up and Thursday at 6:30 doesn't work, you'll shoot me a text the day before, right? Cool. Appreciate you."

You are not begging. You are **professionalizing the appointment.** This dramatically reduces no-shows.

## 14. ARC OBJECTION HANDLING FRAMEWORK

Every objection runs through **ARC**:

**A — Acknowledge**
You agree with the *feeling*, never the *content.* You take the heat out.

**R — Reframe**
You shift the angle. New context. New meaning.

**C — Continue (or Close)**
You re-loop back into the conversation. You do not stop on the objection — you flow past it.

**Example:**
> Homeowner: "I'm not interested."

- **A:** "Totally fair — and most people say that before they even hear what it is, so I get it."
- **R:** "All I'm sharing is that Duke is raising rates again, and most of your neighbors are getting their power for significantly cheaper. That's it."
- **C:** "What's the lowest your bill gets in the year?"

You **never argue the objection.** You absorb it, reframe it, and **continue the original flow.** The objection becomes a speed bump, not a wall.

**Golden Rules**
- Never say "but." "But" erases the acknowledgment. Use "and."
- Never raise your voice. The first one to escalate loses.
- Always end your rebuttal with **a question.** Questions return control to you.
- If they object 3 times to the same thing → it's not the real objection. Find the real one.

## 15. TOP 25 OBJECTIONS — LOOPS & REBUTTALS

For each: **A / R / C.**

### 1. "I'm not interested."
- **A:** "Totally fair — and honestly most folks say that before they hear what it is."
- **R:** "I'm not selling anything door-to-door — I'm just letting the neighborhood know Duke's raising rates again."
- **C:** "What's the lowest your bill gets?"

### 2. "I rent."
- **A:** "Got it — appreciate you telling me."
- **R:** "Quick favor — do you know if the owner lives nearby, or anyone on the street who owns?"
- **C:** Move on.

### 3. "I already have solar."
- **A:** "Oh nice — congrats. When did you get it put in?"
- **R:** "Cool — we actually help a lot of folks with older systems get them rewired or expanded if their bill is still creeping up. What's your true-up running you?"
- **C:** Qualify; if true bill is high, continue. If not, move on warmly.

### 4. "I'm too busy / Now's not a good time."
- **A:** "Totally — and that's actually why I led with 'I don't have much time' — I'll be 90 seconds."
- **R:** "Quick thing — Duke's raising rates again. Most of your neighbors are paying way less now."
- **C:** "What's the lowest your bill gets?"

### 5. "I need to talk to my spouse."
- **A:** "100% — that's the only way we do this anyway."
- **R:** "We always sit down with both of you at the same time, so nobody's playing telephone. When does [spouse] usually get home?"
- **C:** Appointment close: "Tuesday at 6 or Thursday at 7:30?"

### 6. "How much does it cost?"
- **A:** "Great question — and it's the right one to ask."
- **R:** "It actually costs *less* than what you're paying Duke right now — that's the whole point. The numbers are different for every house though, depending on your usage and roof. That's why we'd want to look at the bill before quoting anything."
- **C:** "What's the lowest your bill gets?"

### 7. "I don't want panels on my roof / They're ugly."
- **A:** "Yeah, I get that — that *used* to be a thing."
- **R:** "The new panels are all-black, flush-mount, you barely see them from the street. We can actually pull up your roof on satellite and show you exactly where they'd go."
- **C:** "What's the lowest your bill gets?"

### 8. "I've heard bad things about solar."
- **A:** "Yeah — and honestly some of it's deserved. Some companies have done a bad job."
- **R:** "That's exactly why we're here in the neighborhood — to do it the right way. We're local, we install, we service, and we're not going anywhere."
- **C:** "Real quick — what's the lowest your bill gets?"

### 9. "I'm moving soon."
- **A:** "Got it."
- **R:** "How soon? Because if it's over 6 months, solar actually *increases* your home value, and we can structure it so it transfers or pays off at close."
- **C:** "When are you guys looking to sell?"

### 10. "I don't have good credit."
- **A:** "Totally fine — appreciate you being straight up."
- **R:** "We have a few different programs, some that aren't credit-based at all. Worth letting our consultant just take a quick look."
- **C:** Appointment close.

### 11. "My roof is old / I need a new roof."
- **A:** "Good — that's important to know."
- **R:** "We can actually roll a new roof into the project so it's all one monthly payment, and it gets done before the panels go on. A lot of folks use solar as the reason they finally get the roof handled."
- **C:** "How old is the roof currently?"

### 12. "I don't trust door-to-door."
- **A:** "I don't blame you — I'd be skeptical too."
- **R:** "That's why I'm not asking you to sign anything today. All I want is for you and [spouse] to see your real numbers, side by side with Duke. Then you decide. If it's not a fit, we shake hands and I'm gone."
- **C:** Appointment close.

### 13. "Just send me information / email me."
- **A:** "I would — and here's the thing."
- **R:** "Every house is totally different. The numbers depend on your roof, your usage, your shading. So I literally can't email anything useful — that's why we just sit down for 25 minutes with the real bill."
- **C:** "Does Tuesday at 6 or Thursday at 7:30 work better?"

### 14. "I don't have time tonight."
- **A:** "Totally — I'm not asking for tonight."
- **R:** "I'm just trying to find 25 minutes later this week when both of you are home."
- **C:** "Better earlier in the evening or later?"

### 15. "My bill isn't that high."
- **A:** "Got it — what is it running you?"
- **R:** *(if low)* "Yeah, you might not be a fit honestly." *(disqualify warmly, ask about neighbors)* — *(if mid/high)* "Okay so it's higher than you think — solar typically makes the most sense in your range."
- **C:** Continue or politely exit.

### 16. "I'm on a fixed income."
- **A:** "Yeah — and that's actually the exact reason most of our customers do this."
- **R:** "Because Duke is going to keep raising rates, but a fixed solar payment locks your power cost in. So your bill stops being a moving target."
- **C:** "What's the lowest your bill gets?"

### 17. "Solar doesn't work in this state."
- **A:** "Yeah, you'd be surprised — that's a really common one."
- **R:** "We've got hundreds of homes in this exact zip code producing way more than they use. Germany has half our sun and runs on solar — we get plenty here."
- **C:** "What's the lowest your bill gets?"

### 18. "I'll think about it."
- **A:** "Of course — I'd want to think about it too."
- **R:** "What I've found is 'I'll think about it' usually means there's one specific thing on your mind. What's the main thing?"
- **C:** Handle the *real* objection.

### 19. "Just leave me a card."
- **A:** "Happy to."
- **R:** "And while I'm here — quick question, what's the lowest your Duke bill gets?"
- **C:** Re-enter the flow.

### 20. "I had a bad experience with another solar company."
- **A:** "I'm sorry — that's actually super common and it makes my job harder, honestly."
- **R:** "What happened? *(listen)* Yeah — that's exactly why we do it different. We're local, we install, we service. You'll have my personal number."
- **C:** Continue.

### 21. "Why are rates even going up?"
- **A:** "Great question."
- **R:** "Infrastructure. The grid is old, storms keep taking it out, and they're burying lines now. That's all being passed through to ratepayers — you."
- **C:** "And no one's happy paying more for the same power, right?"

### 22. "I'll wait til the technology gets better."
- **A:** "Smart thinking — that used to be true."
- **R:** "Panels are basically at their efficiency ceiling now. Waiting just means more years of Duke rate hikes you don't get back. The math gets *worse* by waiting, not better."
- **C:** "What's the lowest your bill gets?"

### 23. "Do I really save money?"
- **A:** "Fair question — and it's the only one that matters."
- **R:** "Yes — if we can't show you a real savings on paper, we won't even pitch you. That's literally how we run it."
- **C:** "Let's grab the bill and see if you qualify."

### 24. "Are you with Duke?"
- **A:** "No — we're independent."
- **R:** "We just help homeowners get out from under Duke's rate hikes. We're licensed, local, and you'd own your power instead of renting it forever."
- **C:** "What's the lowest your bill gets?"

### 25. "Just no. Thanks."
- **A:** "All good — appreciate your time."
- **R:** *(walking away, light)* "Quick favor — anybody on the street you know whose bill is killing them? I'll go talk to them instead."
- **C:** Referral or move to next door.

## 16. REJECTION, STATE MANAGEMENT & ENERGY

Rejection is the job. The job is rejection. Once you make peace with that, everything changes.

**The Rule of 7 Seconds**
After a hard no, you have **7 seconds** to reset your state before the next door. Use them:
1. Exhale fully. Drop the shoulders.
2. Smile (even alone — it triggers the state).
3. One affirmation: "Next."
4. Walk taller to the next door than you did to the last one.

**State Triggers That Work**
- **Physical:** Push-ups between streets. Shake your hands out. Slap your thighs once.
- **Mental:** A short trigger phrase. ("I'm the guy." "I'm here on purpose." "Next door, same energy.")
- **Auditory:** Music between blocks if it helps. But OFF before the door.

**Avoid the Death Spiral**
3 hard NIs in a row will tank a rookie. Veteran move:
- After 3 NIs → **change something.** Adjust your opener, switch sides of the street, do 10 pushups, drink water.
- Do not change the *script.* Change the *state.*

**Energy Management Across an 8-Hour Day**
- **Hour 1:** Calibration. Don't expect fireworks. Warm up.
- **Hour 2–4:** Prime time. Your best work. Push.
- **Hour 5:** Mid-day dip. Eat protein, hydrate, walk it off, get back in.
- **Hour 6–8:** Money hour. Most people are home. Most setters are tired. Don't be most setters. **The last hour is where champions are made.**

**Confidence Building**
Confidence isn't a feeling. It is a **byproduct of evidence.** Stack evidence:
- Hit 80 doors. That's evidence.
- Run a clean pitch even on an NI. Evidence.
- Roleplay every morning. Evidence.

Confidence is downstream of standards. Hit your standards daily; confidence comes free.

## 17. SETTER-TO-CLOSER TRANSITION

Your sit is only as good as the handoff.

**The Bridge Phone Call (Live, At the Door or Table)**

> "Hey [Closer], this is [Homeowner] — they've been with Duke for [X] years, bill's been crushing them especially in summer. Both of them are here, kitchen table, ready to see numbers. I told them you're the best at breaking down the math. They're awesome people — take care of them."

**Why this works:**
- You **third-party endorsed** the closer ("the best at breaking down the math").
- You **third-party endorsed** the homeowner ("awesome people"). Now they feel they have to live up to your description.
- You **summarized the pain** so the closer doesn't re-dig it.
- You **anchored the agenda** ("see numbers") — no surprises.

**Handoff Hygiene**
- Stay in the room during the first 5 minutes of the closer's pitch. Then politely exit.
- Do NOT undermine the closer. Do NOT interrupt. Do NOT correct.
- If the homeowner asks you a question mid-close, defer: "Yeah great question — [Closer] is gonna walk you through that, that's his world."

**For Scheduled Appointments**
- Text the homeowner same evening: confirmation + your photo + closer's name + time.
- Day-of, send a reminder text 3–4 hours before. Friendly. "Looking forward to it tonight at 6:30!"
- If the homeowner ghosts → you reschedule, not the closer. You own the appointment until they sit.

## 18. ROLEPLAYS & DRILLS

20 minutes every morning. Non-negotiable. Pair up.

**Drill 1 — The Opener Loop (5 min)**
- Partner A delivers lines 1–4 only. Partner B is neutral homeowner.
- Goal: tonality, eyebrow flash, pointing motion, "does that make sense?" landing softly.
- Switch every 60 seconds.

**Drill 2 — The Disqualifier & Bill Range (5 min)**
- Partner B gives a bill number. Partner A reacts ("Wowww."), digs pain with 2 follow-up questions.
- Coach for: pace, "wowww," genuine curiosity, no machine-gunning.

**Drill 3 — Objection Gauntlet (5 min)**
- Partner B throws 5 random objections back to back.
- Partner A runs ARC each time.
- Coach for: no "but," ending with a question, calm tonality.

**Drill 4 — The Appointment Close (5 min)**
- Partner A runs the full close: spouse qualifier, time options, bill, phone, lock-in.
- Coach for: assumptive language, double-binds, lock-in tightness.

**Weekly Drill — Live Recording**
- Record yourself running a full pitch. Listen back same evening.
- Listen for: filler words ("um," "like," "you know"), upward inflection, pace, energy dips.

## 20. COMMON MISTAKES (AND HOW TO FIX THEM)

| Mistake | Why It Kills You | The Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Talking too fast | Triggers salesman alarm | Pace **slower** than the homeowner |
| Standing too close | Triggers threat response | Stay 4–5 feet from the door |
| Holding the tablet up like a shield | Reads as "salesman" | Tablet at your side or behind your back |
| Pitching with upward inflection | Sounds uncertain | Statements go *down* at the end |
| Skipping the powerline point | Loses the visual anchor | Always point. Always pause. |
| Saying "solar" too early | Triggers defenses | Say *power* and *bill* until they're inside |
| Asking permission ("Can I…?") | Gives them an exit | Use assumptions ("What I'd love to do is…") |
| Arguing objections | Triggers reactance | Run ARC every time |
| Pitching numbers without both HOs | Wastes the sit | Set the appointment, don't pitch alone |
| Open-ended appointment times | Gets "I'll let you know" | Always double-bind two specific times |
| Eating energy on bad doors | Tanks the day | 7-second reset, walk taller to next |
| Quitting at hour 5 | Misses the money hour | The last hour wins the day |
| Not logging doors | Can't fix what you don't track | Log every door — knocked, NI, sit, set |
| Skipping morning roleplay | Cold reps on a cold field | 20 min every morning, paired |

## 21. LEADERSHIP EXPECTATIONS & ACCOUNTABILITY

The team rises with the leader. If you're a senior setter, team lead, or manager, your job is not to pitch *more* — it's to **make everyone else pitch better.**

**Daily Leadership Cadence**

- **Morning huddle (15 min):** Numbers from yesterday. Today's standard. One tactical focus (e.g., "Today we sharpen the disqualifier"). One mindset point.
- **Mid-day check-in (live, 5 min per rep):** Where are you on doors? What's working? What's stuck?
- **End-of-day debrief (20 min):** Numbers in. Wins shared. Losses analyzed. Tomorrow's plan. Energy reset before everyone leaves.

**Accountability System**

| Metric | Tracked Daily | Posted Publicly |
|---|---|---|
| Doors knocked | ✅ | ✅ |
| Quality conversations | ✅ | ✅ |
| Sits | ✅ | ✅ |
| Sales | ✅ | ✅ |
| Roleplay attendance | ✅ | ✅ |

The board is up. Everyone sees everyone's numbers. There is no hiding. Public accountability lifts the floor and pulls the standard up.

**The Three-Strike Standard**
- Miss your standard once → coaching conversation.
- Miss it twice → ride-along for a full day with the team lead.
- Miss it three times → it's a fit conversation. We don't carry passengers.

**What Great Leaders Do Daily**
- **First on the field. Last off.**
- **Roleplay with the newest rep, not just the top rep.**
- **Catch people doing things right.** Public praise > private correction.
- **Own the energy of the team.** If the team is flat, that's a leadership issue, not a rep issue.
- **Tell the truth fast.** Don't let bad habits compound silently.
