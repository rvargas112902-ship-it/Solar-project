# Elite Appointment Setter Handbook  
## Door-to-Door Solar Team Operating System

---

## 1) Mission and Standard

This handbook is your field operating system. It is designed for **immediate execution**, not theory.

**Team minimum standards (non-negotiable):**
- **80 doors/day minimum**
- **2 sits or 1 sale/day minimum standard**
- **Mindset is 95%, skills are 5%**

If your mindset is weak, your script collapses. If your mindset is strong, your script compounds.

---

## 2) Core Setter Identity

You are not a "solar explainer."  
You are a **decision guide** whose job is to:
1. Lower resistance
2. Build relevance
3. Expose financial pain
4. Create a clear next step (sit now or booked appointment)

You win by being:
- Calm under pressure
- Direct without aggression
- Friendly without being needy
- Authoritative without being pushy

---

## 3) Daily Field Rhythm (High Performance Routine)

## Morning routine (30-45 minutes)
1. **State activation (5 min):** breathwork + posture + voice warmup.
2. **Identity review (5 min):**  
   - "I create value at every door."  
   - "Rejection is data, not danger."  
   - "One great conversation changes my day."
3. **Script reps (10 min):** opener, pain questions, appointment close.
4. **Objection reps (10 min):** rapid-fire ARC loops.
5. **Target commitment (2 min):** write exact goals: doors, sits, sales.
6. **Partner lock-in (3 min):** accountability text to leader.

## Pre-turf entry (3 minutes in car)
- Shoulders back, chin neutral, relaxed jaw.
- Smile lightly (not performative, just open).
- Repeat: **"First 10 doors = warmup. Stay in rhythm."**

## Energy management through the day
- Every 25 doors: 2-minute reset (hydration, breath, posture).
- After hard rejection: 10-second emotional reset:  
  **Name it -> release it -> next door.**
- Never carry a previous homeowner's emotion to the next door.

---

## 4) Door Approach Psychology

People decide how to treat you in the first 2-4 seconds.

## Approach mechanics
- Walk with purpose, not speed.
- Knock/ring, then take **one half-step back**.
- Keep hands visible. One hand free, one hand on tablet/folder low.
- Body at slight angle (not squared up like confrontation).
- Eye contact + quick smile when door opens.

## First impression frame
You are there because there is a real local issue, not because you're "trying to sell."
- Use **environmental anchor** (powerlines, neighborhood project, storm outages).
- Lead with brevity and urgency, not hype.

## 5) Core Pitch (Team Script, Field-Ready)

Use this exactly until your fundamentals are automatic.

> **Opener + context anchor**  
> "Hey listen, I don't have much time. Do you see these powerlines here?"  
> *(Point at powerlines)*  
> "They are all going underground because most people lost power during the storms. That way if something catastrophic happens, we'll still have power. Does that make sense?"

> **Rate increase bridge**  
> "Duke is increasing rates to pay for all that infrastructure. No one is happy about paying more money for the same amount of power."

> **Value transition**  
> "If we can get you all the power you're currently using from Duke for significantly cheaper, we'll show you exactly what it looks like."

> **Qualification / disqualification frame**  
> "Most of your neighbors are only paying $35-$40 so we probably can't help them. What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?"

### Why this works
- "I don't have much time" lowers guard and signals confidence.
- Pointing at physical infrastructure creates credibility.
- "Does that make sense?" creates first micro-agreement.
- "We probably can't help them" creates reverse pressure and qualification.

## 6) Tonality System (How You Say It Is the Sale)

## Core tonalities
1. **Calm authority** (default): relaxed, clear, grounded.
2. **Concerned certainty** (rate hikes / storms): serious but not fear-based.
3. **Curious investigator** (bill and pain questions): interested, not interrogative.
4. **Assumptive logistics** (setting sit/appointment): practical and direct.

## Tonality cues by line
- "Hey listen, I don't have much time..." -> **brief, clipped confidence**
- "Does that make sense?" -> **slight upward inflection, soft**
- "No one is happy..." -> **empathetic, shared frustration**
- "What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?" -> **neutral curiosity**
- Time options close -> **matter-of-fact certainty**

## Tonality mistakes
- Too loud = triggers defense.
- Too fast = sounds scripted.
- Too soft = low authority.
- Upward inflection on every line = uncertainty.

## 7) Body Language and Presence Coaching

## Do:
- Keep chest open, shoulders relaxed.
- Nod when they speak to reward engagement.
- Use precise, small gestures (not frantic hands).
- Hold eye contact 60-70% of the time.

## Don't:
- Lean into doorway.
- Fidget with badge/tablet.
- Cross arms.
- Shift weight constantly.

## Rule:
**Stillness = confidence.**  
Move only when transitioning a point.

---

## 8) Micro-Agreements and Conversation Control

You control the conversation by stacking tiny "yes" moments.

## Micro-agreement ladder
1. "Does that make sense?"
2. "You guys were here during those storms, right?"
3. "Fair to say higher bills aren't your favorite thing either?"
4. "Let me ask you this..."

Each yes lowers resistance and earns the next question.

## Control without sounding controlling
- Use **question-led direction**, not commands.
- Replace "You need to..." with "What most homeowners are doing is..."
- Keep transitions smooth:
  - "Real quick..."
  - "Out of curiosity..."
  - "So I can see if this even makes sense for you..."

---

## 9) Pain-Building Framework (Natural, Not Pushy)

Pain-building is not manipulation. It is helping them **accurately feel** what they are already paying.

## Framework: Surface -> Magnify -> Personalize -> Contrast

### 1) Surface
"What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?"

### 2) Magnify
"And what does it spike to in summer?"

### 3) Personalize
- "Wowww." *(Pause. Let it land.)*  
- "Do you guys have a pool or big family here?"
- "How long have you been seeing bills like that?"
- "What else would that extra money ideally go toward?"

### 4) Contrast
"So if you're already paying that every month anyway, you'd at least want that payment working for you instead of climbing every year, right?"

## Emotional deepening prompts
- "What's most frustrating: summer spikes, unpredictability, or just the yearly increases?"
- "If rates keep climbing the next few years, how annoying does that get for you?"
- "What would a stable lower payment do for your monthly budget?"

---

## 10) Bill Transition and Usage Offset Explanation

## Asking for the bill (confident + normal)
"Perfect. To see if this is worth your time, grab your latest Duke bill real quick. I'll show you in 60 seconds if you're even a fit."

If resistance:
"Totally fair. Without the bill we'd just be guessing, and I don't want to waste your time."

## How to explain offset with the "squiggly line"
When looking at usage graph:
1. Point to usage trend line ("squiggly line")
2. Explain simply:
   - "This line is your monthly energy usage pattern."
   - "Our goal is to offset this usage so you're buying less and less from Duke."
   - "It's not about using less power; it's about paying less for the power you already use."

Keep it visual, brief, and practical.

---

## 11) Qualification Rules (Fast Filters)

## Likely good fit
- Monthly bill regularly above local threshold
- Homeowner(s) involved in decisions
- Roof/site looks viable (basic visual check)
- Frustrated with rising utility rates

## Likely low-priority lead
- Very low bill consistently ($35-$40 range)
- Not decision-maker
- No urgency and no pain

Use disqualification frame professionally:
"You may already be in the low-usage group where this doesn't move the needle enough, but let's verify quickly with the bill."

---

## 12) In-Home Transition vs Appointment Set

Your job at the door is a clear next step:
- **If both homeowners are available and qualified -> go inside for a sit now**
- **If not -> lock a two-homeowner appointment**

## Transition to sit now
"You both are here now, perfect. Let's hop inside for 10-15 minutes so I can map this to your actual usage and show exact numbers."

## If one homeowner is missing
"Got it. Since both of you are involved in home decisions, let's set a time when you're both here so you only do this once."

---

## 13) Appointment Closing System (No Open-Ended Closes)

## Step-by-step appointment close
1. **Decision-maker check:**  
   "Are both homeowners involved in the utility/solar decision?"
2. **Set frame:**  
   "Perfect, this only works if both are there."
3. **Time-option close (never 'when are you free?'):**  
   "Is today at 6:30 better, or tomorrow at 5:45?"
4. **Confirm commitment:**  
   "Great, I have you at Tuesday 5:45 with both homeowners."
5. **Secure contact + bill photo:**  
   "Best number for confirmation text?"  
   "Send me a quick photo of your latest bill so we prep accurately."
6. **Reduce no-show risk:**  
   "If anything shifts, just text us early so we can adjust."

## Final lock line
"Awesome, this is just a numbers walkthrough to see if it saves you money. If it doesn't, we shake hands and you're done."

## 14) ARC Objection Handling Framework

Use ARC on every objection.

## A = Acknowledge
Validate emotion without agreeing with false assumptions.  
"Fair." "Totally get that." "Makes sense."

## R = Reframe
Shift from emotion to process/value.  
"That's exactly why we look at your real usage first..."

## C = Close with a micro-commitment
Ask a small forward-moving question.  
"If it saves you money, worth 10 minutes to verify?"

### ARC example
Homeowner: "Not interested."
- **A:** "Totally fair."
- **R:** "Most people say that before seeing how much Duke has gone up."
- **C:** "Real quick, what's your average bill range now?"

## 15) Common Objections, Loops, and Rebuttals

## 1) "Not interested."
- "Makes sense. Most people aren't interested in 'solar,' they're interested in paying less than Duke. Are you at least open to seeing if the numbers work?"

## 2) "I don't have time."
- "Same here, that's why I'll keep this to 60 seconds at the door. If it's a fit, we schedule properly. If not, we stop."

## 3) "I need to talk to my spouse."
- "Exactly. That's why we only meet when both are there. Is tomorrow at 5:45 or 6:30 better?"

## 4) "I heard solar is a scam."
- "You're right to be cautious. That's exactly why we use your actual Duke bill and show transparent numbers first. Want to see if your home even qualifies before judging it?"

## 5) "We might move."
- "Good to bring up. Depending on timeline, this may or may not make sense. Let's review options in the appointment so you can make a clean decision."

## 6) "My roof is old/shaded."
- "Could be disqualifying, and if so we'll tell you fast. Let's first confirm usage and fit, then site details."

## 7) "I already have solar."
- "Nice. Then this may be about optimization or storage depending on your offset and rate plan. Mind if I ask what your true-up looked like last cycle?"

## Loop rule
After every rebuttal, return to a forward question:
- "Fair enough—what does your bill usually peak at?"
- "Would you rather compare now or keep guessing?"

## 18) Common Setter Mistakes (and Corrections)

1. **Talking too much before qualification**  
   - Fix: ask bill question by line 4-6.
2. **Pitching features before pain**  
   - Fix: pain first, solution second.
3. **Asking open-ended appointment questions**  
   - Fix: always either/or time options.
4. **Skipping decision-maker check**  
   - Fix: confirm both homeowners early.
5. **Losing frame after first objection**  
   - Fix: ARC + loop back to question.
6. **Needy energy**  
   - Fix: disqualify posture ("might not be a fit").

## 19) Best Practices That Raise Conversion

- Keep door interactions short and directional.
- Use local references (storms, outages, neighborhood updates).
- Ask better questions; don't deliver longer monologues.
- Praise transparency when homeowners share bill info.
- Use silence after key pain points ("Wowww...").
- Confirm every commitment out loud.
- Send confirmation text before leaving the driveway.

## 20) Setter-to-Closer Handoff (No Drop-Off System)

A strong sit can die with a weak handoff. Setters must transfer context clearly.

## Required handoff info
- Homeowner names
- Best phone number
- Both decision-makers confirmed (yes/no)
- Lowest and highest monthly bill
- Emotional pain trigger (what bothered them most)
- Objections already surfaced
- Appointment time lock
- Bill photo secured (yes/no)

## Handoff script to closer (internal)
"They are at $160 low / $300 high, biggest pain is summer unpredictability. Both homeowners confirmed for 5:45. Skeptical about scams, but open to transparent numbers."

## 21) Rejection Recovery and Emotional State Control

## The 3R reset after rejection
1. **Recognize:** "That was a no, not a personal attack."
2. **Release:** one deep breath, relax jaw/shoulders.
3. **Re-engage:** next door within 20 seconds.

## Mental rules
- Never celebrate too high or crash too low.
- Measure yourself by standards executed, not one conversation.
- Confidence is a behavior before it is a feeling.
