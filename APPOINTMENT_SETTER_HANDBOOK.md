# Elite Appointment Setter Handbook
## Door-to-Door Solar Team Operating System

---

## 1) Mission and Standard

This handbook is for field execution. The goal is not to sound impressive. The goal is to create enough trust and curiosity for the homeowner to take the next step.

**Team minimum standards:**
- **80 doors/day minimum**
- **2 sits or 1 sale/day minimum standard**
- Track doors, conversations, bill pulls, sits, and sales daily.

Your job at the door is to:
1. Lower resistance fast.
2. Make the conversation feel local and relevant.
3. Find out whether the homeowner has enough utility pain to continue.
4. Create a clear next step: sit now or booked appointment.

---

## 2) Setter Role

You are not a "solar explainer." You are a **decision guide**.

Do not lead with panels, tax credits, technical details, or a long company story. Lead with what the homeowner already understands:
- Power outages
- Utility rate increases
- Monthly bill pain
- Paying more for the same usage

The best setters are:
- Brief
- Calm
- Conversational
- Curious
- Clear about the next step

---

## 3) Daily Field Rhythm

Keep preparation practical and short.

## Before knocking
1. Confirm turf, route, and tracking method.
2. Review the opener, bill question, and appointment close.
3. Set numbers for the day: doors, conversations, bill pulls, sits.
4. Confirm who receives handoffs and what info they need.

## In the field
- Knock with pace. Do not overthink each door.
- After every 25 doors, check your numbers and adjust.
- If you get rejected, move to the next door quickly.
- Do not carry the last homeowner's mood into the next conversation.

## End of day
- Report doors, conversations, bill pulls, sits, sales, no-shows.
- Identify the one part of the pitch that needs reps tomorrow.

---

## 4) Door Approach Psychology

Homeowners decide whether you are a threat, nuisance, or normal person in the first few seconds.

## Approach mechanics
- Walk with purpose, not speed.
- Knock/ring, then take one half-step back.
- Keep hands visible.
- Stand at a slight angle instead of squared up to the doorway.
- Give a quick, normal smile when they open.
- Start talking before awkward silence builds.

## First impression frame
Your first job is to make the homeowner think:

> "This person is not here to trap me in a long sales pitch."

That is why the opener is short, local, and specific.

---

## 5) Core Pitch

Use the structure exactly until it becomes natural. Do not perform it like a script. Understand the reason behind each line so you can say it like a real conversation.

> **Opener + time release**
> "Hey listen, I don't have much time."

> **Environmental anchor**
> "Do you see these powerlines here?"
> *(Point at the powerlines or local infrastructure.)*

> **Local reason for stopping**
> "They are all going underground because most people lost power during the storms. That way if something catastrophic happens, we'll still have power. Does that make sense?"

> **Rate increase bridge**
> "Duke is increasing rates to pay for all that infrastructure. No one is happy about paying more money for the same amount of power."

> **Value transition**
> "If we can get you all the power you're currently using from Duke for significantly cheaper, we'll show you exactly what it looks like."

> **Disqualification + bill question**
> "Most of your neighbors are only paying $35-$40 so we probably can't help them. What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?"

---

## 6) Pitch Breakdown: Why Each Line Works

## Line 1: "Hey listen, I don't have much time."

**Why we say it:**
- It breaks the normal salesperson pattern.
- It tells the homeowner this will not be a long conversation.
- It creates a small role reversal: you are not begging for their time.

**Psychological effect:**
- Lowers their guard because they expect a salesperson to ask for time.
- Creates curiosity because you sound like you are there for a specific reason.
- Makes you appear busy and selective instead of needy.

**How to say it:**
- Calm and quick, like you are catching them before moving on.
- Do not sound rushed or frantic.
- Do not overemphasize it. It should feel casual.

**Common mistake:**
- Saying it like a memorized pickup line. If it sounds fake, it creates distrust immediately.

---

## Line 2: "Do you see these powerlines here?"

**Why we say it:**
- It moves the conversation from "random salesperson" to "something happening right here."
- It gets the homeowner looking at the same physical reference as you.
- It creates a simple answer they can give without pressure.

**Psychological effect:**
- Shared attention creates instant relevance.
- The homeowner feels like the conversation is about their neighborhood, not a generic solar pitch.
- It earns the right to explain the reason you stopped.

**How to say it:**
- Point naturally.
- Pause long enough for them to look.
- If there are no visible powerlines, use another local anchor: road work, storm damage, outage history, or neighborhood utility updates.

**Homeowner reaction you want:**
- "Yeah."
- A nod.
- Looking where you point.

That small response is the first micro-agreement.

---

## Line 3: "They are all going underground because most people lost power during the storms."

**Why we say it:**
- It gives the conversation a local reason.
- It ties the pitch to a problem the homeowner already remembers.
- It keeps the focus on infrastructure, reliability, and cost instead of "buy solar."

**Psychological effect:**
- Triggers memory of storms, outages, inconvenience, and frustration.
- Makes the topic feel practical instead of salesy.
- Builds credibility because you are talking about something visible and local.

**How to say it:**
- Serious but not dramatic.
- Do not use fear-based language.
- If they did not lose power, pivot: "Some homes got lucky, but the upgrade is still being paid for across the area."

---

## Line 4: "That way if something catastrophic happens, we'll still have power. Does that make sense?"

**Why we say it:**
- It summarizes the infrastructure upgrade in plain language.
- "Does that make sense?" checks understanding and creates agreement.
- It lets the homeowner participate without feeling interrogated.

**Psychological effect:**
- The homeowner says or signals "yes," which makes the conversation easier to continue.
- It makes you feel reasonable because you are checking in instead of lecturing.
- It slows the pitch enough to feel conversational.

**How to say it:**
- Say the explanation simply.
- Ask "Does that make sense?" softly, not like a challenge.
- If they say no, clarify in one sentence and move on.

---

## Line 5: "Duke is increasing rates to pay for all that infrastructure."

**Why we say it:**
- It connects the local upgrade to the homeowner's bill.
- It explains why rates are going up without sounding like a conspiracy.
- It creates the financial reason for the conversation.

**Psychological effect:**
- Moves the homeowner from curiosity to personal relevance.
- Makes them think about their own monthly payment.
- Creates a problem that solar may solve without pitching solar yet.

**How to say it:**
- Matter-of-fact.
- Do not sound angry at Duke. The point is not to rant; the point is to identify the cost pressure.

---

## Line 6: "No one is happy about paying more money for the same amount of power."

**Why we say it:**
- It names the frustration in a way most homeowners agree with.
- It makes you sound like you are on their side.
- It converts a utility-rate topic into an emotional pain point.

**Psychological effect:**
- Creates an "us vs. the problem" feeling.
- Gives them permission to complain about the bill.
- Makes the next question about their bill feel natural.

**How to say it:**
- Empathetic, not dramatic.
- Watch their face. If they react, ask a follow-up: "Have you noticed yours creeping up too?"

---

## Line 7: "If we can get you all the power you're currently using from Duke for significantly cheaper, we'll show you exactly what it looks like."

**Why we say it:**
- It presents the offer as a conditional fit check, not a guaranteed promise.
- It focuses on the same power they already use, just at a lower cost.
- It creates a reason to look at their bill.

**Psychological effect:**
- Reduces perceived risk because you are not asking them to buy anything at the door.
- Creates curiosity about whether they qualify.
- Frames the next step as information, not pressure.

**How to say it:**
- Slow down slightly.
- Emphasize "if" and "exactly."
- Do not overpromise savings. The bill determines whether it makes sense.

---

## Line 8: "Most of your neighbors are only paying $35-$40 so we probably can't help them."

**Why we say it:**
- It uses disqualification instead of chasing.
- It tells the homeowner not everyone is a fit.
- It makes the conversation feel selective.

**Psychological effect:**
- Lowers resistance because you are not assuming they should buy.
- Creates reverse pressure: people want to know if they are in the group that qualifies.
- Prevents you from wasting time with low-bill homes.

**How to say it:**
- Neutral and casual.
- Do not insult low bills.
- Do not sound disappointed if they are low usage.

---

## Line 9: "What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?"

**Why we say it:**
- It qualifies quickly.
- Asking for the lowest bill feels less invasive than asking for the highest bill first.
- Once they answer low, it becomes natural to ask what it spikes to.

**Psychological effect:**
- Moves the conversation from theory to their actual numbers.
- Gets the homeowner talking about their situation.
- Opens the door to pain-building without a long pitch.

**How to say it:**
- Curious, like you are checking fit.
- After they answer, pause. Let the number sit.
- Then ask: "And what does it usually hit in the summer?"

## 7) How to Avoid Sounding Like a Robot

The words matter, but the conversation matters more. A homeowner can tell when you are reciting.

## Use the script as a map, not a mask
- Keep the order of the pitch.
- Keep the key phrases.
- Change small connector words so it sounds like you.
- React to what the homeowner says before going to the next line.

## Add short acknowledgments
Use quick, natural responses:
- "Exactly."
- "That makes sense."
- "Yeah, that's what most people here are saying."
- "Got it."
- "Fair."

Then continue the structure.

## Match their pace
- If they are short and guarded, stay brief.
- If they are friendly, allow a little warmth but do not lose control.
- If they are skeptical, slow down and ask more questions.
- If they are busy, shorten the pitch and ask the bill range question faster.

## Do not talk over reactions
If they laugh, complain, nod, or mention their bill, respond to that moment. That is where rapport is built.

Example:

Homeowner: "Yeah, Duke has been killing us."

Setter: "That's exactly why I stopped. When it gets bad, where does your bill usually land?"

That sounds human and still moves the conversation forward.

## 8) Rapid Rapport: Lower Their Guard Fast

Rapport at the door does not mean becoming best friends. It means making the homeowner feel safe enough to keep talking.

## The fast rapport formula
1. **Release pressure:** "I don't have much time."
2. **Use the environment:** "Do you see these powerlines?"
3. **Create agreement:** "Does that make sense?"
4. **Share their frustration:** "No one is happy about paying more."
5. **Ask a fit question:** "What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?"

## What lowers their guard instantly
- Standing back from the door.
- Speaking in a normal volume.
- Giving them an easy first question.
- Making the reason local.
- Not asking for money, signatures, or a decision at the door.
- Using "if" language: "If it makes sense..." "If we can help..."

## What raises their guard
- Launching into a full solar explanation.
- Talking too fast.
- Ignoring their first objection.
- Acting overly excited.
- Asking for an appointment before creating relevance.
- Sounding like you need the sale.

## Quick rapport lines based on the pitch
- "You probably saw the same outages everyone else did."
- "Most people are less annoyed about the work and more annoyed that rates keep climbing."
- "I'm not sure if it even makes sense here yet. That's why I ask the bill question first."
- "If your bill is already low, I'll tell you straight up this probably is not worth your time."

## 9) Tonality System

## Core tonalities
1. **Calm authority:** relaxed, clear, grounded.
2. **Local concern:** serious about outages/rates without fear tactics.
3. **Curious investigator:** interested in their bill, not interrogating.
4. **Assumptive logistics:** practical and direct when setting the next step.

## Tonality by line
- "Hey listen, I don't have much time." - brief and casual.
- "Do you see these powerlines?" - observational, not theatrical.
- "Does that make sense?" - soft check-in.
- "No one is happy..." - empathetic, shared frustration.
- "What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?" - neutral curiosity.
- Time options close - matter-of-fact certainty.

## Tonality mistakes
- Too loud = defensive homeowner.
- Too fast = scripted and low trust.
- Too soft = low authority.
- Too excited = salesperson alarm.
- Same tone on every line = robotic.

## 10) Conversation Control

You control the conversation by asking short questions that move the homeowner one step at a time.

## Micro-agreement ladder
1. "Do you see these powerlines?"
2. "Does that make sense?"
3. "Have you noticed your bill moving up too?"
4. "What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?"
5. "And what does it spike to in summer?"

Each answer earns the next question.

## Keep transitions smooth
- "Real quick..."
- "Out of curiosity..."
- "So I can see if this even makes sense..."
- "That is exactly why I ask..."
- "Before I waste your time..."

## Avoid open-ended wandering
Do not ask:
- "What do you think about solar?"
- "Are you interested in solar?"
- "When are you free?"

Ask:
- "What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?"
- "Is today at 6:30 better, or tomorrow at 5:45?"

---

## 11) Pain-Building Framework

Pain-building is not manipulation. It is helping the homeowner connect the rate conversation to what they already pay.

## Framework: Surface -> Magnify -> Personalize -> Contrast

### 1) Surface
"What's the lowest your Duke bill gets?"

### 2) Magnify
"And what does it spike to in summer?"

### 3) Personalize
- "Wow. How long has it been hitting that range?"
- "Do you guys have a pool, older AC, or a bigger family here?"
- "Is it the summer spike or the constant increase that bothers you more?"

### 4) Contrast
"So if you're already paying that every month anyway, you'd at least want to see if that payment could be lower and more predictable, right?"

## Keep it natural
Do not stack five questions like an interrogation. Ask one, listen, respond, then ask the next.

---

## 12) Bill Transition and Usage Offset Explanation

## Asking for the bill
"Perfect. To see if this is worth your time, grab your latest Duke bill real quick. I'll show you in 60 seconds if you're even a fit."

If resistance:
"Totally fair. Without the bill we'd just be guessing, and I don't want to waste your time."

## Explain usage simply
When looking at the usage graph:
1. Point to the monthly usage trend.
2. Say:
   - "This line is your monthly energy usage pattern."
   - "Our goal is to offset this usage so you're buying less from Duke."
   - "It's not about using less power. It's about paying less for the power you already use."

Keep it visual, brief, and practical.

---

## 13) Qualification Rules

## Likely good fit
- Monthly bill is consistently above the local threshold.
- Homeowner or decision-makers are involved.
- Roof/site looks viable from a basic visual check.
- Homeowner is frustrated with rising utility rates.

## Likely low-priority lead
- Very low bill consistently ($35-$40 range).
- Not the decision-maker.
- No urgency and no bill pain.

Use disqualification professionally:

"You may already be in the low-usage group where this doesn't move the needle enough, but let's verify quickly with the bill."

---

## 14) In-Home Transition vs Appointment Set

Your job at the door is a clear next step:
- **If both homeowners are available and qualified:** go inside for a sit now.
- **If not:** lock a two-homeowner appointment.

## Transition to sit now
"You both are here now, perfect. Let's hop inside for 10-15 minutes so I can map this to your actual usage and show exact numbers."

## If one homeowner is missing
"Got it. Since both of you are involved in home decisions, let's set a time when you're both here so you only do this once."

---

## 15) Appointment Closing System

Do not use open-ended closes.

## Step-by-step close
1. **Decision-maker check:**  
   "Are both homeowners involved in the utility/solar decision?"
2. **Set frame:**  
   "Perfect, this only works if both are there."
3. **Time-option close:**
   "Is today at 6:30 better, or tomorrow at 5:45?"
4. **Confirm commitment:**  
   "Great, I have you at Tuesday 5:45 with both homeowners."
5. **Secure contact + bill photo:**  
   "Best number for confirmation text?"  
   "Send me a quick photo of your latest bill so we prep accurately."
6. **Reduce no-show risk:**  
   "If anything shifts, just text us early so we can adjust."

## Final lock line
"Awesome, this is just a numbers walkthrough to see if it saves you money. If it doesn't, we shake hands and you're done."

## 16) ARC Objection Handling Framework

Use ARC on every objection.

## A = Acknowledge
Validate the concern without agreeing with a false assumption.

Examples:
- "Fair."
- "Totally get that."
- "Makes sense."

## R = Reframe
Move from the objection back to the reason for the conversation.

Example:
"That's exactly why we look at your real usage first."

## C = Close with a micro-commitment
Ask a small forward-moving question.

Example:
"If it saves you money, worth 10 minutes to verify?"

## ARC example
Homeowner: "Not interested."

Setter:
- **A:** "Totally fair."
- **R:** "Most people aren't interested in solar. They're interested in seeing if Duke can be beaten on price."
- **C:** "Real quick, are your bills usually under $50 or over $150?"

---

## 17) Common Objections, Loops, and Rebuttals

## 1) "Not interested."
"Makes sense. Most people aren't interested in solar; they're interested in paying less than Duke. Are you at least open to seeing if the numbers work?"

## 2) "I don't have time."
"Same here, that's why I'll keep this to 60 seconds at the door. If it's a fit, we schedule properly. If not, we stop."

## 3) "I need to talk to my spouse."
"Exactly. That's why we only meet when both are there. Is tomorrow at 5:45 or 6:30 better?"

## 4) "I heard solar is a scam."
"You're right to be cautious. That's exactly why we use your actual Duke bill and show transparent numbers first. Want to see if your home even qualifies before judging it?"

## 5) "We might move."
"Good to bring up. Depending on timeline, this may or may not make sense. Let's review options in the appointment so you can make a clean decision."

## 6) "My roof is old/shaded."
"Could be disqualifying, and if so we'll tell you fast. Let's first confirm usage and fit, then site details."

## 7) "I already have solar."
"Nice. Then this may be about optimization or storage depending on your offset and rate plan. Mind if I ask what your true-up looked like last cycle?"

## Loop rule
After every rebuttal, return to a forward question:
- "Fair enough, what does your bill usually peak at?"
- "Would you rather compare now or keep guessing?"
- "Before I waste your time, are you usually above or below $150?"

## 20) Common Setter Mistakes and Corrections

1. **Talking too much before qualification**
   - Fix: ask the bill question by line 4-6.
2. **Sounding memorized**
   - Fix: react to the homeowner before moving to the next line.
3. **Pitching solar features before bill pain**
   - Fix: rate pressure and bill first, product second.
4. **Asking open-ended appointment questions**
   - Fix: always use either/or time options.
5. **Skipping decision-maker check**
   - Fix: confirm both homeowners early.
6. **Losing frame after the first objection**
   - Fix: ARC, then loop back to a bill or appointment question.
7. **Needy energy**
   - Fix: use fit-check language: "I'm not sure this makes sense yet."

## 21) Best Practices That Raise Conversion

- Keep door interactions short and directional.
- Use local references: storms, outages, powerlines, utility work.
- Ask better questions instead of giving longer explanations.
- Listen for emotional cues: frustration, confusion, skepticism, urgency.
- Respond to what they say before continuing the script.
- Use silence after important bill numbers.
- Confirm every commitment out loud.
- Send confirmation text before leaving the driveway.

---

## 22) Setter-to-Closer Handoff

A strong sit can die with a weak handoff. Transfer context clearly.

## Required handoff info
- Homeowner names.
- Best phone number.
- Both decision-makers confirmed (yes/no).
- Lowest and highest monthly bill.
- Emotional pain trigger.
- Objections already surfaced.
- Appointment time lock.
- Bill photo secured (yes/no).

## Handoff example
"They are at $160 low / $300 high. Biggest pain is summer unpredictability. Both homeowners confirmed for 5:45. They were skeptical about scams, but open to transparent numbers."

---

## 23) Rejection Recovery

Rejection is part of the numbers. Do not turn one "no" into five bad doors.

## Reset
1. Identify what happened: brush-off, real objection, or bad fit.
2. Capture the lesson if there is one.
3. Go to the next door within 20 seconds.

Measure execution, not emotion.
