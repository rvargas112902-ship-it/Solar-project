# LightReach Solar Lease Customer Guide

Use this as a customer conversation guide for the Palmetto LightReach Florida solar lease. It is written to surface every point that may scare a homeowner, then turn it into an honest, fact-based explanation. This is not legal advice; it is a sales and education guide.

## Quick deal facts from this contract

- Product: Palmetto LightReach solar lease, Florida version 4.1.1.
- Term: 25 years / 300 monthly payments.
- Monthly payment: $204.32.
- Annual escalator shown in the executed payment schedule: 0%.
- Upfront payment: $0.
- Total lease payments: $61,296.
- System size: 13.760 kW DC.
- Estimated first-year production: 23,351 kWh.
- Estimated 25-year production: 466,921 kWh.
- Estimated lease cost per produced kWh if the production estimate is met: about 13.1 cents/kWh.
- Utility listed: LCEC.
- Added product: full-service backup battery marked as included, with $0.00 listed as the portion of the initial monthly payment attributable to additional products.

## Part 1: Contract outline

### 1. The basic promise

LightReach owns, installs, insures, monitors, maintains, repairs, and operates the solar system. The homeowner pays a fixed monthly lease payment and uses the solar energy produced by the system.

### 2. Installation and interconnection

LightReach or its certified installer handles design, permits, approvals, installation, and utility interconnection. The contract warns that utility approval can take 1 to 3 months or more because interconnection depends on the utility and other outside approvals.

### 3. Payments

The customer pays $204.32 per month for 300 months, totaling $61,296. The first payment starts after utility interconnection and invoicing. Automatic payment is assumed; paying by another method can add $15/month, and late payments can trigger a $25/month late fee after the grace period.

### 4. Ownership, incentives, and tax credits

This is a lease, not a purchase. LightReach owns the system, the tax credits, rebates, RECs, capacity rights, and system data. The homeowner receives the use of the system and reduced utility consumption, not ownership of the equipment or incentives.

### 5. Warranty, maintenance, and repairs

The system warranty and repair promise run for the full lease term. LightReach covers covered defects, workmanship, replacement parts, labor, monitoring, and system repairs. Roof penetrations are warranted for at least 10 years, and the system is insured by LightReach except for homeowner-caused damage, gross negligence, or failure to follow maintenance obligations.

### 6. Homeowner obligations

The homeowner must keep trees and obstructions from shading the system, protect the system from debris and animal damage, maintain internet for monitoring, avoid modifying the system, give reasonable access for service, cooperate with utility paperwork, keep utility service active, and notify LightReach before home sale or utility changes.

### 7. Battery terms

The contract includes full-service backup battery language, but it clearly says backup power is not guaranteed without interruption, may only support designated circuits, should not be used for life-support or medical equipment, and cannot be modified or paired with another generation source without approval.

### 8. Selling the home

The lease can be transferred if the buyer meets LightReach underwriting requirements and signs the transfer documents. The buyer receives the same lease rate. If the buyer cannot qualify, the seller may need another solution such as prepayment, purchase request, or LightReach-approved alternative.

### 9. Purchase, prepayment, renewal, and end of term

The homeowner may request to purchase the system after year 5, after year 5 at any time, or when selling the home, but LightReach must agree and the price is fair market value. The homeowner may prepay remaining monthly payments at a 5% discount. At the end of the lease, the customer may renew, request removal, or potentially receive the system as-is if LightReach does not remove, sell, or renegotiate and the customer does not request removal within the stated window.

### 10. Default, disputes, and legal terms

Default can occur after missed payments, material nonperformance, unauthorized transfer, false information, bankruptcy-type events, or unauthorized system changes. Remedies can include termination, disconnection, collection of unpaid and remaining payments, credit reporting, removal costs, and other legal remedies. Most disputes go to individual arbitration, not class action or jury trial.

## Part 2: What may scare a homeowner

### 1. "I am signing for 25 years."

Yes, this is a long contract. That can feel intimidating because it lasts longer than many car loans, personal loans, and even some homeowner plans.

### 2. "I do not own the panels."

Correct. The homeowner leases the system. They do not own the equipment, tax credit, RECs, or other incentives.

### 3. "The total payments are $61,296."

The total number can look large because it is the full 25-year total, not a cash price due today.

### 4. "I will still have a utility bill."

Yes. The customer will usually receive a utility bill and a LightReach bill. The utility bill can include fixed charges, taxes, non-bypassable charges, and energy used beyond solar production.

### 5. "Savings are not guaranteed."

The contract gives production estimates and warranty obligations, but it does not guarantee a specific dollar savings result because utility rates, household usage, weather, shading, interconnection rules, and net metering rules can change.

### 6. "The tax credit goes to LightReach."

That is true because LightReach owns the system. The customer should not expect to claim the federal solar tax credit on a leased system.

### 7. "Selling my home could be harder."

The buyer must assume the lease and meet underwriting requirements, or another solution must be arranged. This is one of the biggest emotional objections for homeowners.

### 8. "There is a UCC filing."

LightReach may file a UCC-1 notice showing that it owns the system. The contract says this is intended as notice of ownership, not a lien against the home, but homeowners often worry when they hear "filing."

### 9. "I cannot cancel after installation starts."

The homeowner has a 10-day cancellation right, and may cancel before installation starts subject to the contract terms. After installation begins, the right to terminate becomes very limited.

### 10. "If I default, the remedies are serious."

Missed payments or major contract violations can lead to fees, credit reporting, disconnection, collection of future rent, system removal, and legal remedies.

### 11. "I have to maintain trees, internet, and access."

The homeowner must keep shade and obstructions under control, maintain internet for monitoring, and allow reasonable service access.

### 12. "Roof repairs could cost me."

If the homeowner needs roof work or renovations that require removing and reinstalling the system, that removal and reinstallation can be at the homeowner's expense unless covered by warranty.

### 13. "The battery is not a whole-home, never-fail generator."

The contract says backup battery power is not guaranteed during every outage and may only power designated circuits. It is not a life-support solution.

### 14. "LightReach can assign the contract."

LightReach can sell, assign, or transfer the agreement to a financing partner without homeowner consent, although the maintenance and warranty obligations remain.

### 15. "There is arbitration and no class action."

Most disputes must be handled through individual arbitration or small claims, not a jury trial or class action.

### 16. "The contract uses a lot of legal language about data and privacy."

LightReach can collect and use system data, usage patterns, and related information for operations, incentives, monitoring, and business purposes.

### 17. "Florida disclosures mention liens and insurance."

Florida-required disclosures warn homeowners about construction lien law, insurance, roof age, and cancellation rights. These warnings are standard consumer-protection disclosures, but they can sound alarming.

### 18. "Interconnection can be delayed."

The contract says utility review can take 1 to 3 months or more, so the customer should not expect instant activation after installation.

## Part 3: Turning each concern into a positive, with facts and logic

### 1. Long 25-year term

**Positive frame:** "The length is what lets the payment stay low and fixed. This is not a short-term gadget; it is infrastructure on the home."

Solar leases commonly last 20 years or more, and the FTC specifically tells homeowners to review long-term solar leases carefully rather than treating long terms as unusual. The Department of Energy also notes that lease and PPA arrangements commonly include installer maintenance responsibilities. Sources: FTC, "Solar Power for Your Home" (https://consumer.ftc.gov/articles/solar-power-your-home); DOE, "Planning a Home Solar Electric System" (https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/planning-home-solar-electric-system).

**Best customer line:** "The term is long because the system is designed to produce for decades. The question is not 'Is 25 years long?' The question is 'Would you rather keep buying 100% of your power from the utility with no 25-year price protection?'"

### 2. No ownership

**Positive frame:** "Not owning it is the point: you avoid the equipment risk."

With ownership, the homeowner owns the upside but also owns the maintenance responsibility, inverter replacement risk, monitoring burden, and warranty management. With this lease, LightReach owns the equipment and is responsible for covered operation and maintenance for the term.

The FTC states that leased solar is company-owned and the leasing company will probably be responsible for system maintenance. DOE also says lease/PPA arrangements commonly require the installer to provide maintenance. Sources: FTC (https://consumer.ftc.gov/articles/solar-power-your-home); DOE (https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/planning-home-solar-electric-system).

**Best customer line:** "This is like choosing a protected energy subscription instead of buying and maintaining a power plant on your roof."

### 3. $61,296 total lease payments

**Positive frame:** "That is the 25-year total, not today's cost. Today's cost is $0 upfront and $204.32/month."

The better comparison is not the total against zero. The real comparison is 25 years of solar lease payments plus remaining utility charges versus 25 years of utility bills with no system, no battery benefit, no service plan, and no fixed solar payment.

If the system produces the estimated 466,921 kWh, the lease payment math equals about 13.1 cents per produced kWh before utility fixed charges and taxes. LCEC's 2026 residential tariff lists a $20/month customer charge, tiered energy rates up to 12.82 cents/kWh over 1,000 kWh, and a power cost adjustment of 3.215 cents/kWh, meaning many kWh from the grid can be around the mid-teens before other charges. Florida's 2025 average residential electricity price was 17.30 cents/kWh according to EIA. Sources: LCEC 2026 rates (https://www.lcec.net/my-account/rates/); EIA Electric Power Monthly Table 5.3 (https://www.eia.gov/electricity/monthly/epm_table_grapher.cfm?t=epmt_5_3).

**Best customer line:** "The contract turns an unknown utility expense into a known solar payment. With this schedule, the solar payment is flat instead of escalating."

### 4. Still having a utility bill

**Positive frame:** "Solar offsets usage; it does not disconnect the home from the grid."

Florida net metering rules credit excess generation against future usage, but customers still pay applicable fixed customer charges and other utility charges. LCEC also lists a monthly customer charge that applies regardless of energy usage. Sources: Florida net metering rule summary via FSEC (https://energyresearch.ucf.edu/consumer/energy-policy/interconnection-net-metering/); LCEC rates (https://www.lcec.net/my-account/rates/).

**Best customer line:** "You are not replacing the utility company; you are buying less from them."

### 5. No guaranteed dollar savings

**Positive frame:** "The contract is honest because no one controls future utility rates, weather, shading, or household usage."

FTC warns homeowners to check whether savings are guaranteed and to understand assumptions. The contract discloses that estimates are estimates, which protects the customer from exaggerated promises. Sources: FTC (https://consumer.ftc.gov/articles/solar-power-your-home).

**Best customer line:** "A serious contract shows the assumptions. We sell this by comparing the customer's real usage and real utility rate, not by pretending the future is guaranteed."

### 6. Tax credits belong to LightReach

**Positive frame:** "That is how a $0-upfront lease works."

The FTC and DOE both explain that if a homeowner leases a system, they do not own it and generally cannot claim tax credits or incentives; those go to the system owner. The tradeoff is that the owner uses incentives, financing, and scale to offer a lower monthly lease without the homeowner writing a large check. Sources: FTC (https://consumer.ftc.gov/articles/solar-power-your-home); DOE (https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/planning-home-solar-electric-system).

**Best customer line:** "If you want the tax credit, ownership may be the better product. If you want $0 upfront, included service, and no equipment ownership risk, the lease is designed for that."

### 7. Selling the home

**Positive frame:** "This contract has a defined transfer path instead of leaving the seller to figure it out."

The buyer can assume the same lease rate if approved. The contract also says LightReach will not prohibit sale, conveyance, or refinancing, and will explain the UCC filing to purchasers, lenders, and title companies upon request. That matters because the objection is usually uncertainty, not the existence of solar.

Owned solar has been shown to add value in resale studies; Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory found average premiums around $4/W or $15,000 for an average 3.6 kW owned PV system. That specific study applies to owned systems, not leases, but it proves buyers value lower energy costs when the economics are clear. Source: Berkeley Lab, "Selling Into the Sun" (https://emp.lbl.gov/publications/selling-sun-price-premium-analysis).

**Best customer line:** "The sale conversation is simple: the buyer gets the same fixed solar payment, covered maintenance, and the benefit of buying less utility power."

### 8. UCC filing

**Positive frame:** "A UCC notice protects ownership of the panels; it is not meant to be a mortgage on the home."

The contract says the UCC-1 is intended only to give notice of LightReach's rights in the system and is not a lien or encumbrance against the home. Florida's disclosure form also distinguishes a UCC-1 notice from a home lien. Source: Florida Statute 520.23 disclosure requirements (https://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&Search_String=&URL=0500-0599%2F0520%2FSections%2F0520.23.html).

**Best customer line:** "It is like a title record for equipment LightReach owns, not a claim that LightReach owns the house."

### 9. Cancellation limits

**Positive frame:** "The contract gives a real cooling-off window, then protects both sides once expensive work begins."

Florida law requires at least a 3-business-day rescission right for distributed energy generation system agreements. This contract gives 10 days, which is more customer-friendly than the statutory minimum. Source: Florida Statute 520.23 (https://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&Search_String=&URL=0500-0599%2F0520%2FSections%2F0520.23.html).

**Best customer line:** "You have time to review. Once installation starts, LightReach has already committed equipment, labor, engineering, permits, and financing."

### 10. Serious default remedies

**Positive frame:** "The remedies are strong because LightReach owns expensive equipment on the property for 25 years."

The practical way to explain this is simple: pay on time, do not damage or alter the system, communicate before selling, and keep the account in good standing. A lease has remedies because the homeowner gets use of a high-value asset with $0 upfront.

**Best customer line:** "The contract is strict about default, but the path to staying safe is straightforward: make the payment, do not modify the system, keep access available, and call LightReach before major home or sale decisions."

### 11. Tree, internet, and access obligations

**Positive frame:** "These obligations protect production and service."

Solar production depends on sunlight and monitoring. NREL research shows PV systems typically degrade gradually over time, but avoidable issues like shade, outages, and lack of monitoring can create preventable production losses. Source: NREL PV fleet degradation research via OSTI (https://www.osti.gov/pages/biblio/1864619).

**Best customer line:** "LightReach can only stand behind the system if it can see the system, access the system, and keep the panels exposed to sun."

### 12. Roof repair removal costs

**Positive frame:** "This is why roof condition should be discussed before signing."

Florida's standard disclosure warns that homeowners should consider roof age because roof replacement may require removing and reinstalling the solar system. This is not unique to LightReach; it is a real-world solar issue. Source: Florida Statute 520.23 disclosure framework (https://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&Search_String=&URL=0500-0599%2F0520%2FSections%2F0520.23.html).

**Best customer line:** "If the roof is healthy, the lease adds protection. If the roof is near replacement, solve that first so the customer does not pay twice."

### 13. Battery limitations

**Positive frame:** "The battery is backup support, not a hospital-grade generator."

The contract's battery warning is a safety disclosure. It prevents the customer from expecting unlimited whole-home power or life-support reliability. The honest benefit is resilience for designated circuits, better energy management, and included service, not a guarantee that every appliance runs forever during every outage.

**Best customer line:** "A battery is for resilience and smarter energy use. It is not a promise that the grid can fail for days and the home behaves like nothing happened."

### 14. Assignment to financing partners

**Positive frame:** "Assignment is how large solar companies finance $0-upfront systems."

Third-party ownership models commonly use financing partners. SEIA explains that the developer finances, installs, owns, and maintains the system while receiving payments and incentives. Source: SEIA overview of third-party solar finance/PPA model (https://seia.org/research-resources/solar-power-purchase-agreements/).

**Best customer line:** "The name behind the financing can change, but the customer's payment schedule and service obligations stay governed by the contract."

### 15. Arbitration and class-action waiver

**Positive frame:** "This is common in consumer finance contracts, and small claims remains available for individual relief."

This is still a legitimate customer concern. The right way to sell it is not to minimize it, but to explain that the agreement uses AAA consumer arbitration procedures and preserves small-claims options for individualized disputes.

**Best customer line:** "If dispute rights are the customer's biggest concern, they should review this section carefully. For most customers, the bigger day-to-day protection is the written warranty and service obligation."

### 16. Data and privacy

**Positive frame:** "Monitoring data is how LightReach detects problems and keeps the system performing."

The system needs production and usage data for monitoring, troubleshooting, incentive qualification, and optimization. The customer should understand that data sharing exists, but the practical purpose is service and energy management.

**Best customer line:** "The monitoring that feels invasive on paper is also what lets them catch issues instead of waiting for the customer to notice a bad bill."

### 17. Florida lien and insurance disclosures

**Positive frame:** "Those disclosures are consumer protections required because home improvement work is serious."

Florida law requires solar lease disclosures covering ownership, cost, payments, UCC notices, transfer terms, warranties, roof issues, insurance, and cancellation rights. The warning language is designed to force transparency before signing. Source: Florida Statute 520.23 (https://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&Search_String=&URL=0500-0599%2F0520%2FSections%2F0520.23.html).

**Best customer line:** "The scary disclosures are there because Florida wants homeowners informed. A transparent contract is better than a simple contract hiding the hard parts."

### 18. Interconnection delays

**Positive frame:** "The delay is usually utility process, not the system failing."

Florida interconnection and net metering rules involve applications, utility review, metering, and approval. The contract discloses that timing depends on third parties. Source: FSEC interconnection and net metering overview (https://energyresearch.ucf.edu/consumer/energy-policy/interconnection-net-metering/).

**Best customer line:** "Install day is not the finish line; permission to operate is. We set expectations early so the customer is not surprised."

## Highest-impact customer close

"This contract is not for someone who wants to own panels, chase tax credits, and manage equipment for 25 years. It is for a homeowner who wants $0 upfront, a flat $204.32 solar payment, a large 13.760 kW system, battery support, included monitoring, included maintenance, and protection from relying only on utility power. The honest tradeoff is commitment: keep the home in good standing, communicate before selling, and understand that utility bills do not disappear. If those tradeoffs are acceptable, the contract is much less scary than it looks."

## Questions to ask the customer before they decide

1. How long do you realistically expect to stay in the home?
2. Is your roof in good shape for the next 10+ years?
3. What is your average monthly utility bill over the last 12 months?
4. Are you comfortable with two bills if the combined bill is lower or more predictable?
5. Do you prefer ownership and tax credits, or $0 upfront and included maintenance?
6. Are you comfortable transferring the lease if you sell?
7. Which circuits do you expect the battery to support during an outage?
8. Do you understand that solar reduces utility usage but does not eliminate fixed utility charges?
9. Are all blank spaces and payment terms completed before signing?
10. Do you want an attorney or tax professional to review ownership, tax-credit, arbitration, and resale terms?
